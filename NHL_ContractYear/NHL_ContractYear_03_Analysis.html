4. Filtering and Preparing Data for Analysis

To ensure robust within-player comparisons, this section applies two key filters:

  • a minimum playing-time requirement and
  • a requirement that players must have both contract-year and non-contract-year seasons

In this section, we will create the final analytic dataset for all performance comparisons. The goal of this filtering is to ensure that every player has enough meaningful playing time and at least one contract-year season and one non-contract-year season, allowing us to make valid comparisons within each player's stats.

4.1 Minimum Games Played Filter

To remove player-seasons with extremely limited playing time, we'll apply a minimum threshold of 25 games played. This cutoff balances sample size with reliability. 25 games represents approximately 30% of the season, so the cutoff helps to eliminate partial and injury-shortened seasons that could otherwise distort the player performance metrics, while still including seasons with meaningful statistics.

5. Arranging Player-Level Data for Analysis

Before we can compare contract-year vs non-contract-year performance, we need to restructure the season-level dataset into a player-level format. Each player will have a single row containing their average stats in contract-year seasons and non-contract-year seasons.
This section will:

  • Create a pivot table separating contract and non-contract averages
  • Add a performance_tier column (low, mid, high) based on each player's average points per 60 minutes across all seasons
  • Allow us to quickly filter, group, and reference player statistics in later analyses

Instead of storing one row per season, we'll create a pivot table that summarizes each player's average performance in contract-year seasons and non-contract-year seasons.

5.2 Categorizing Players by Performance Level

Next, we'll categorize players by their overall skill level. While we could use different stats for forwards and defensemen, we decided to use points_per_60 as a single metric for all players. This keeps the analysis simple, consistent, and avoids the noise inherent in plusMinus.

Players will be grouped into three categories based on performance: Stars (top 25%), Mid-Level (middle 50%), and Lower-Tier (bottom 25%). This will allow us to clearly distinguish elite players from the middle and lower tiers.

Rather than ranking players season by season, we'll calculate each player's average points_per_60 across all seasons. This average will determine their tier, and the result will be stored in a new column, performance_tier in the df_final dataframe for analysis.

In[22]:
# Create a dataframe that calculates each player's average points_per_60 across all their seasons
player_avgs = df_final.groupby("playerId")["points_per_60"].mean().reset_index()

q25 = player_avgs["avg_points_per_60"].quantile(0.25)
q75 = player_avgs["avg_points_per_60"].quantile(0.75)

# Assignment function

6. Analyzing Performance Differences Between Contract and Non-Contract Seasons

In this section, we compare each player's performance in contract-year seasons to their performance in non-contract-year seasons. All analyses will be based on within-player differences rather than comparisons across players.
We will:

  • Calculate the average difference (delta) between contract-year and non-contract-year statistics for each player
  • Compute standard deviations to assess whether observed differences exceed expected variability
  • Visualize the distributions of differences to see patterns, or lack thereof
  • Perform subgroup analyses by player position, performance tier, etc
  • Analyze time on ice per game to determine whether coaches are influenced by players' contract year

6.1 Comparing Contract vs Non-Contract Performance

Now that we've built our pivot table containing each player's average performance during contract and non-contract seasons, we can begin comparing how players perform in their contract years.

To quantify this, we'll calculate a delta for each performance statistic.
delta = (contract-year average) (non-contract-year average)

We'll take the average delta value per stat for each player, reflecting how their output changes when they are in a contract season.
We then compute the overall mean delta (are players better or worse in their contract years on average?) and the standard deviation (how much natural variation exists across all players?).

While there are slight differences in contract vs non-contract deltas between the performance tiers, all values remain well within the overall standard deviations. This indicates that there is no statistically meaningful differences in seasonal performance across tiers.

6.3 Performance Differences by Position (Forwards vs Defensemen)

At this point, the overall analysis, as well as our performance-tier subgrouping, has shown no statistically significant difference between contract-year and non-contract year performance or time on ice. All observed deltas fall well within normal season-to-season variability.

To complete our subgroup comparison, we'll now examine whether this pattern holds when players are separated by position. Since forwards and defensemen have fundamentally different roles, scoring rates, and usage patterns, it is still possible that contract-year effects might manifest differently between the two groups.

We will divide the dataset into forwards (C, LW, RW) and defensemen (D) and compute contract vs non-contract deltas for each group in the same manner as previous sections. We will consider defensemen to be any player that includes "D" in their positionCode column for 50% or more of their seasons.

7. Overall Conclusion

Across all analyzed statistics, our findings were very consistent. For the tested metrics of points per 60 minutes, plus/minus points, and time on ice per game, the differences between contract-year and non-contract-year seasons for players were small, inconsistent in direction, and statistically insignificant.

When breaking players into finer subgroups, such as high, mid, and low level performers or forwards vs defensemen, no category showed a meaningful contract-year performance difference. All observed differences were well within one standard deviation, indicating the fluctuations are indistinguishable from normal player-to-player and season-to-season variability.

Taken as a whole, the results provide strong evidence against the common belief that NHL players elevate their performance in contract years, or that coaches, believing this, might put these players out on the ice for more minutes per game. Performance appears relatively stable, and coaching decisions regarding ice time do not seem influenced by contract status.

8. Study Limitations and Recommendations for Further Analysis

8.1 Study Limitations

While this analysis provides a thorough exploration of contract-year performance effects, several limitations should be kept in mind:

  1. Data Scope
    • The dataset examines only a range of 13 seasons from 2012/2013 to 2024/2025
    • Only regular season games were included
    • Some more advanced performance metrics (e.g., expected goals, Corsi/Fenwick, RAPM) were not included
    • Goalies were excluded entirely
    • Performance data was retrieved from the NHL's publicly available statistics interface (unofficial API access)
    • Contract year data was scraped Sportrac, combining information from three separate tables: Signed Free Agents, Available Free Agents, and Contract Extensions
  2. Methodological Simplifications
    • Contract-year identification is binary and does not distinguish between Restricted and Unrestricted free agents or contract length
    • Team and line effects (quality of teammates, zone starts, usage context) were not controlled for
    • Simple within-player averages were used rather than any more complex statistical models
    • Contract and non-contract averages were compared across a player's entire set of seasons, rather than only proximal seasons
  3. Potential Noise in Seasonal Stats
    • Single-season plus/minus can be influenced heavily by team and line strength
    • Points per 60 minutes can be influenced heavily by injuries, team and line strength, and special team usage

8.2 Recommended Next Steps

Further analysis could explore:

  • Regression-based models to isolate contract-year effects while controlling for team, age, and usage
  • Separate analyses for UFS vs RFA contract years
  • Incorporating advanced analytics for more granular performance insight
  • Studying goalie performance
  • Long-term trends, such as whether contract-year incentives differ across eras
  • Comparing proximal seasons directly (comparing a contract year only to the immediately preceding seasons, rather than to all non-contract years combined)
